Opportunity #1 — Talent & People · Skills & Development
Staffroom
AI-powered educator performance and professional development intelligence for multi-site school networks
Opp. Score
45

Multi-site school operators are drowning in educator appraisal cycles they cannot process at scale. Catholic Schools Parramatta Diocese manages 3,500–5,000 staff across 80+ campuses through spreadsheet-based appraisal tracking that does not connect to NESA registration databases or professional learning records. PHINMA runs 74,000 students across distributed Philippine campuses with no holding-company HR analytics layer — its Director of IT has no CTO peer to drive integration. The problem is not that these organisations lack ambition; it is that education-specific HR schemas (teaching area, accreditation status, professional learning hours, pastoral observation notes) are invisible to generic HRIS copilots. Staffroom ingests fragmented HR exports, appraisal documents, and registration data, normalises them against national teaching standards (AITSL, DepEd, CHED), and surfaces a ranked principal dashboard: who needs a development conversation, who is a flight risk, whose certification lapses in the next 90 days.

The Moat

Salesforce HR Cloud and Workday do not encode AITSL graduate-to-proficient-to-highly-accomplished teaching standards, DepEd RPMS appraisal matrices, or the concept of a teaching area scarcity premium. They also require a full IT integration project that a 15-person school IT team cannot execute. The moat here is schema depth (education-specific ontology built in) and deployment model (CSV upload or lightweight API connector to TechOne/FACES/Coursera, not a months-long ERP integration). The second moat is corpus: each customer's appraisal data trains institution-specific risk signals that improve over time and cannot be replicated by a competitor starting fresh.

Entry Path

Phase 1 — Consulting engagement (4–6 weeks, $25–40K): Map HR data sources, appraisal process, and registration databases at one site. Deliver a static risk dashboard as a proof of concept using exported data.
Phase 2 — Pilot (3 months, $8–12K/month retainer): Deploy live-connected Staffroom for 1–2 regions (e.g. CSPD's Blacktown corridor or PHINMA Mapúa Manila). Instrument principal usage, measure appraisal cycle time reduction.
Phase 3 — Product ($3–6K/month per network): Self-service SaaS with standard connectors to TechOne, HRIS exports, and NESA/DepEd APIs. Expansion within network requires no additional sales motion.

Opportunity #2 — Growth & Market · Revenue & Funding Diversification + Market Expansion
Enrolflow
Enrollment intelligence platform — from inquiry signal to cohort yield forecast — built for education organisations in price-sensitive markets
Opp. Score
41

Revenue diversification (heat 57, 5 companies) and market expansion (heat 51, 5 companies) share a common root cause: these organisations cannot predict which students will convert, re-enrol, or drop out before it happens. Rising Academies operates 400+ low-fee private schools in West Africa where the conversion decision happens via SMS and community word-of-mouth, not a CRM funnel. School for Life and WISE both score 78 and 70 respectively on revenue diversification — their funding models are aid-dependent and they need to demonstrate cohort retention to unlock the next grant tranche. iPeople's STEM programs in the Philippines compete against Mapúa (now PHINMA) and ASU partnerships; their acquisition funnel is driven by academic-cycle timing that generic marketing tools do not understand. Enrolflow classifies inquiry signals from SMS, email, and WhatsApp by intent and dropout risk, forecasts cohort yield through the academic calendar, and surfaces intervention queues for admissions staff — no data science team required.

The Moat

HubSpot and Salesforce Education Cloud model a standard SaaS sales funnel — not the West African school enrollment decision, which involves parental income proxies, school-year periodicity, government subsidy eligibility (RA 10931 in the Philippines), and community-level outreach signals. The dropout-risk classifier must be trained on education-specific behavioural patterns (fee payment gaps, attendance dips before re-enrolment windows), not e-commerce churn. The data network effect is significant: cross-institution signal aggregation (opt-in, anonymised) improves forecast accuracy for any individual operator, creating a compounding advantage that a first-mover can sustain.

Entry Path

Phase 1 — Consulting engagement (6–8 weeks, $30–50K): Audit enrollment data pipeline at Rising Academies or PHINMA. Build a cohort yield regression on historical data; present findings to admissions leadership.
Phase 2 — Pilot (4 months, $10–15K/month): Deploy real-time classifier on one intake cycle. Measure actual vs. forecast yield and intervention conversion rate.
Phase 3 — Product ($4–8K/month per institution): Multi-channel inbox connector (WhatsApp Business API, email, SMS gateway) with academic-calendar-aware forecasting engine. LMIC pricing tier at $1–2K/month for organisations in Sub-Saharan Africa.

Opportunity #3 — Financial · Financial Visibility & Planning
GrantLens
Grant compliance and multi-source funding intelligence for education organisations navigating complex government and donor reporting
Opp. Score
37

Financial visibility (heat 51, 5 companies) is the third-ranked opportunity by score, but it is particularly acute for the development-sector and government-funded organisations in this cohort. Acasus operates across 20+ countries advising governments on education reform — its CFO must track grant conditions, restricted vs. unrestricted budget pools, and donor acquittal timelines across dozens of concurrent engagements. School for Life (heat 63 on financial visibility) and Rising Academies (55) depend almost entirely on donor and impact-investor funding; fee default propensity modeling and grant compliance tracking are survival-level requirements, not optimisation plays. PHINMA navigates RA 10931 government tuition subsidies, CHED funding rules, PSE disclosure obligations, and donor grant conditions simultaneously — today this is a manual document-review exercise. GrantLens is a RAG-over-funding-agreements layer: ingest grant agreements, government funding determination letters, audit findings, and donor reporting templates; allow finance staff to query compliance obligations in plain language with cited source passages; and alert on upcoming reporting deadlines and budget utilisation thresholds.

The Moat

Generic document Q&A tools (Notion AI, Microsoft Copilot) have no concept of Commonwealth recurrent grants, CHED-mandated program funding floors, or donor reporting acquittal schedules. The value is not in the RAG technology — it is in the document taxonomy (knowing that a "Programme Memorandum" from DFAT and an "Annual Grant Determination" from the NSW Department of Education are structurally equivalent funding instruments) and the compliance ontology (knowing which clauses trigger reporting obligations vs. spending restrictions). This is a domain knowledge moat that takes two to three client engagements to build and cannot be replicated by a general-purpose tool without similar investment.

Entry Path

Phase 1 — Consulting engagement (3–4 weeks, $15–25K): Audit funding instrument portfolio at School for Life or Acasus. Manually tag compliance obligations and build initial ontology. Deliver a structured compliance calendar as the artefact.
Phase 2 — Pilot (3 months, $6–10K/month): Deploy GrantLens over the live document corpus. Finance staff test Q&A queries; measure time-to-answer vs. baseline manual review.
Phase 3 — Product ($2–4K/month per organisation): Lightweight web application — document upload or Google Drive connector, compliance Q&A interface, deadline alert digest. No ML infrastructure required on client side.

Opportunity #4 — Operations & Delivery · Technology & Infrastructure
SiteIQ
Multi-site operational risk triage for distributed school and campus networks — prioritise central support where it matters most
Opp. Score
19

Technology and infrastructure (heat 52, 5 companies) ranks lower by salience-weighted score because operational problems feel solvable through management effort — but for organisations running 80–400+ sites, that assumption breaks down. Rising Academies cannot fly a central support officer to 400 schools in Sierra Leone, Liberia, and Rwanda to assess which ones need urgent intervention; it needs a ranked risk list. PHINMA (heat 62 on operations) runs distributed campuses from Manila to Cagayan de Oro with a campus-level Director of IT and no holding-company operations analytics. CSPD manages 80+ schools across Western Sydney through a hub-and-spoke model where the central system office is the only oversight layer — and it is currently blind to which schools are heading toward a staffing or welfare incident. SiteIQ aggregates operational signals (attendance data, staff absence rates, maintenance ticket volumes, resource utilisation) from each site, applies a composite risk score, and surfaces a weekly ranked list of sites needing central support. The input is CSV exports or lightweight LMS/SIS API connections — not a custom data warehouse build.

The Moat

Microsoft Power BI and generic BI dashboards can display site-level metrics but cannot rank sites by composite risk or recommend the next action. The moat is the education-specific risk model: the weighting between a staffing gap, a welfare incident backlog, and a resource shortfall is not obvious without domain knowledge — and the model must account for school-year periodicity (term 4 pressure is categorically different from term 1). The secondary moat is deployment model: LMIC school networks cannot run a cloud data warehouse. SiteIQ must work off periodic exports and produce actionable output within minutes, not after a BI engineer configures a pipeline. Honest limitation: Rising Academies' 400-school network in West Africa may lack the structured data collection infrastructure to feed the model reliably — this is better suited to PHINMA and CSPD as initial customers.

Entry Path

Phase 1 — Consulting engagement (4–6 weeks, $20–35K): Identify available operational data sources at PHINMA or CSPD. Define the risk signal taxonomy. Deliver a manual risk-ranked site list as a proof of concept using one term's data.
Phase 2 — Pilot (3 months, $8–12K/month): Automate ingestion from available sources (SIS exports, Google Classroom, TechOne). Weekly risk digest delivered to central operations team by email.
Phase 3 — Product ($2–5K/month per network): Web dashboard with configurable risk weights and alert thresholds. No installation required — CSV upload or lightweight API.

Opportunity #5 — Strategy & Direction · Transformation & Change + Partnerships & Ecosystem
SectorPulse
Sector intelligence and evidence synthesis engine for education leaders — turning policy documents, research, and sector reports into cited strategic briefs
Opp. Score
26

Transformation (heat 52, 6 companies) and partnerships (heat 51, 5 companies) score lower on opportunity because they are high-salience but hard to address with AI directly — leaders acknowledge the problems but prefer human consultants. However, the underlying information problem is tractable: every organisation in this cohort needs to synthesise across program evaluations, government policy white papers, international development reports, and sector intelligence to make strategy decisions — and none of them has a dedicated research function to do it. WISE (score 62 on transformation) is the Qatar Foundation's education convening platform; its staff synthesise OECD reports, UNESCO research, and EdTech market analysis for summit programming. Acasus advises governments in 20+ countries and competes on depth of sector knowledge — its consultants spend significant time reading and cross-referencing policy documents that AI could surface faster. SectorPulse is a RAG-over-sector-corpus tool: ingest curated education research feeds (OECD Education at a Glance, World Bank EdStats, DFAT research series, USAID evaluations), allow leadership teams to ask cross-document strategy questions, and receive cited multi-source briefs rather than single-document summaries.

Phase 3 — Product ($1.5–3K/month per organisation + corpus licensing fee): Subscription access to the curated corpus with custom document upload. WISE as reference customer enables positioning for other education convening bodies (Brookings, T4 Education).

Opportunity #6 — Governance & Leadership · Power Concentration & Transparency + Executive Leadership Capability
BoardBrief
Governance intelligence layer for education boards — turning voluminous committee papers, accreditation evidence, and audit findings into structured executive intelligence
Opp. Score
25

Governance is a joint top heat domain at 52, with power concentration and transparency (heat 56, 6 companies) being the highest-scoring sub-category in the domain. However, the salience weight is 0.7 — governance problems are real but leaders are less likely to frame them as AI-solvable, which suppresses the opportunity score (25 vs. 45 for Staffroom). The underlying structural problem is genuine: PHINMA must satisfy Yuchengco Group, Ayala Corporation, PSE listing obligations, CHED, DepEd, NPC, and ABET simultaneously, generating committee papers that no individual board member can process in full. CSPD operates under Catholic diocesan governance structures with dual accountability to the Bishop of Parramatta and Commonwealth government funding bodies. School for Life (score 68) and Acasus (score 64) operate under impact investor and philanthropic governance where evidence of outcomes is a contractual obligation, not a preference. BoardBrief ingests board packs, committee reports, accreditation evidence matrices, and audit findings — and produces structured governance intelligence: a compliance status dashboard, a decision-by-decision accountability log, and anomaly flags when approval timelines or expenditure authorisation patterns deviate from policy norms.

Phase 3 — Product ($3–6K/month per board): Board pack upload interface, structured compliance dashboard, monthly anomaly digest. Positioned as governance documentation software, not AI analytics.
