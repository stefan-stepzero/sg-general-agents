Company Playbook

This is not a mass pitch. Each company has a different entry point, a different buyer, and a different reason to say yes — or no. The playbook below is tailored to their specific problem profile: what keeps their leadership up at night, which product to lead with, and what to watch out for before you walk in the door.

PHINMA Education

Opening line: "You're managing 177,000 students across 13 campuses, you've just taken PE capital with a digital transformation mandate, and you have a CIO in place — but your workforce data is still living in Mastersoft exports and spreadsheets. The appraisal cycle for 3,500-plus staff is a manual bottleneck your principals hate. We can fix that."

Lead product: Staffroom. PHINMA has the highest staffing complexity of any company in this cohort: licensed healthcare educators across provincial campuses, board-exam performance as a public KPI, and KKR explicitly mandating digital transformation. Staffroom — which normalises HR exports against teaching standards (CHED, PRC) and surfaces a ranked principal dashboard of flight risks, performance gaps, and certification lapses — maps directly onto Chito Salazar's "bare-bones, results-driven" operational philosophy. It shows ROI on a per-campus basis within one semester. EnrolFlow (enrollment funnel forecasting toward the 300,000-student target) is the natural second conversation once Staffroom is bedded in.

Key pain points: (1) Skills & Development (score 62.5) — appraisal cycle management across 13 campuses with 3,500+ staff is a known bottleneck; faculty quality is their primary competitive KPI and the current data infrastructure cannot surface it at speed. (2) Revenue & Enrollment Diversification — funnel visibility from inquiry to enrollment for a 300,000-student growth target is weak; ERP-native reporting cannot segment conversion by campus or cohort. (3) Quality & Consistency — the hub-and-spoke network creates real variance in delivery standards across campuses; no centralised operational risk signal exists to tell HQ which campus needs attention this week.

Buyer: Dr. Chito B. Salazar Jr. (President & CEO) is the strategic entry point — he is the founder-era decision-maker and the one who framed the KKR deal as a digital transformation mandate. CTO Alex Ustaris (ETCIO Transformative CIO 2024) is the operational technology champion and likely the internal sponsor once Salazar green-lights a conversation. Route in through Salazar; close with Ustaris.

Watch out: PHINMA's operating philosophy is "bare-bones, no-frills, brass knuckles" — they will not buy aspirational technology. Every conversation must lead with per-campus ROI and board-exam-cycle timing. They are also a cost-conscious affordable education operator: a pricing model that feels enterprise-SaaS will be dead on arrival. Land with a pilot on 2–3 campuses, prove the number, then expand.

School for Life Foundation

Opening line: "You raised $3 million in a single night, you're running 10 schools with 250 staff in Uganda, and your entire compliance and grant reporting process is someone manually reading documents. With a $3M raise you'll be accountable to donors faster than ever. GrantLens gets that off your desk."

Lead product: GrantLens. School for Life is 100% philanthropically funded — the Gala Ball, RAWCS auspice conditions, ACFID Code of Conduct compliance, and individual major donor reporting are their existential obligations. They have no CRM, no BI tool, and one Platform Architect on staff. GrantLens (RAG over grant agreements, donor reporting templates, and compliance documents) requires zero data engineering and delivers immediate time savings for a small Australian HQ team that is manually managing all of this. The pitch is "your Jess and Annabelle get hours back every week."

Key pain points: (1) Revenue & Funding Diversification (score 63 for growth/diversification pressure) — over 50% of their 2025 fundraising came from a single anonymous donor match; they know this is a concentration risk and are actively seeking to broaden the donor base, which means more compliance strings, not fewer. (2) Financial Visibility & Planning — ACNC reporting, RAWCS board conditions, and ACFID Code compliance are all document-heavy accountability obligations that currently require manual review. (3) Quality & Operational Consistency — managing 10 schools across Mpigi District with 250 staff from a Sydney HQ means the Australian leadership team has almost no real-time visibility into site-level operations; SiteIQ is a natural follow-on once GrantLens is established.

Buyer: Annabelle Chauncy OAM (CEO and Co-Founder) makes every significant decision — this is a founder-led organisation with a small Australian team. Jess Ledieu (Head of Philanthropy Projects) is the operational champion who would use the tool daily and is the right person to run a pilot. Tamara and Lexi (partnerships and corporate outreach) are secondary stakeholders who would benefit from cleaner donor compliance evidence. Come via Annabelle; involve Jess in scoping.

Watch out: This is a mission-driven charity with deep values around community trust and locally-led development. Any pitch that sounds like "data extraction from Uganda" will land badly. Frame entirely around the Australian fundraising and governance burden — the value is giving the Sydney team more time to focus on impact storytelling and donor relationships, not about instrumentalising the Ugandan program. Also: budget is real but modest; the conversation must start with a price that a $3–5M AUD nonprofit can absorb.

iPeople Inc (Mapúa University)

Opening line: "Mapúa just launched the Philippines' first BS AI Engineering degree. You're the AI-forward university in the country. But your enrollment funnel is still running on ERP-native reporting and manual Coursera dashboards. Let's make the data infrastructure match the brand."

Lead product: EnrolFlow. iPeople's primary growth KPI is enrollment — 74,000 students, +10% YoY, targeting further growth across 7 institutions. They have revenue up 18% and earnings up 31% but their funnel analytics (inquiry to application to enrollment) run on a campus SIS that was not built for cross-institution trend analysis. EnrolFlow — enrollment funnel forecasting with education-specific cohort pattern recognition — gives Fred Ayala and the NTC post-merger integration team the conversion visibility they need to allocate recruitment resources across institutions. The Coursera GenAI partnership already gives them 45,000 students generating learning data; EnrolFlow makes that data actionable for admissions planning.

Key pain points: (1) Revenue & Enrollment Diversification (score 68) — cross-institution enrollment forecasting across the Mapúa group, NTC merged entity, and affordable segment is not currently possible from native ERP data. (2) Skills & Development (score 60) — with 1,895 staff across 7+ institutions and ABET faculty qualification requirements as a compliance obligation, workforce analytics is underdeveloped; the Director of IT operates at campus level with no holding-company-level staff data view. (3) Financial Visibility — as a PSE-listed company with quarterly disclosure obligations, the gap between ERP-level SIS data and investor-grade analytics is real and getting more visible as TTM revenue approaches $107M.

Buyer: Fred Ayala (President, COO & Director) is the right entry point — Harvard MBA, operationally in charge, the one who championed Coursera and ASU partnerships. He will understand a product conversation immediately. Dr. Reynaldo Vea (Chairman & CEO, Yuchengco side) is less likely to engage on product specifics. The Director of IT (Joselito Sol Cruz) is the implementation champion. Joselito's team will actually run the pilot; get Fred to say yes, then hand off to Joselito.

Watch out: iPeople is a PSE-listed company with dual controlling shareholders (Yuchengco + Ayala) — significant decisions go through a formal governance process. Fred cannot commit unilaterally; expect a longer sales cycle with a formal procurement review. Additionally, Mapúa's identity as the Philippines' AI leader means they will be skeptical of any product that doesn't live up to a genuine technology standard. Do not oversell; under-promise and over-deliver on the data pipeline quality.

Rising Academies

Opening line: "You're running 900 schools across four countries, you have a $4.25M Series A to double your reach, and you just built an AI tutor on Claude. The problem is your operational data — attendance, coach deployment, resource gaps — is still living in field spreadsheets. You can't scale what you can't see."

Lead product: SiteIQ. Rising's core operational challenge at scale is multi-site visibility: which of the 900 schools is underperforming this week, which needs a coaching visit, which is about to lose a teacher. Their tech infrastructure for the AI products (Rori, Tari) is genuinely impressive — MERN stack, Anthropic API, production ML. But their school operations data layer is conventional field reporting. SiteIQ aggregates operational signals from periodic CSV exports (no data warehouse required) and produces a ranked site-risk list for central teams. This is exactly the constraint they need solved to deliver on the "double student reach" mandate from Klett Group without doubling headcount.

Key pain points: (1) Quality & Operational Consistency (score 74 for risk scoring fit) — 900 schools across Sierra Leone, Ghana, Liberia, Rwanda with a hub-and-spoke field operations model means HQ has no systematic way to triage where to send coaches. (2) Skills & Development (score 55) — teacher attrition in West Africa is a structural constraint; forecasting staffing gaps by school before they become crises is directly on Stephanie Dobrowolski's problem list. (3) Financial Visibility — multiple government contracts (Rwanda MINEDUC, Liberia) plus philanthropic grants create a compliance reporting burden; grant acquittal documentation is a meaningful administrative overhead for a 501–1,000 person organisation.

Buyer: Stephanie Dobrowolski (CEO, Co-Founder) is the day-to-day decision-maker since the 2024 transition. Paul Skidmore (Executive Chairman) is the strategic framing conversation — he speaks fluently about AI and governance. CTO Shabnam Aggarwal is the technical validator; she will assess whether SiteIQ's data architecture is compatible with their WhatsApp-native, low-bandwidth operating environment. Approach Stephanie on the operational problem; Shabnam on the technical fit.

Watch out: Rising is deeply evidence-driven. They ran a peer-reviewed RCT before scaling Rori. They will expect evidence that SiteIQ produces actionable school-level signals, not just pretty dashboards. Have a concrete case — ideally a comparable multi-site LMIC operator — ready. Also: they are pre-profitability on an equity+grant mix; any pricing must be grant-fundable or framed as part of the Klett Series A deployment. Do not pitch enterprise SaaS pricing to a B Corp social enterprise on a Series A runway.

Kinetic Software

Opening line: "You've got 85% of UK universities locked in and a new CEO who joined to grow. The product gap everyone in your market is watching is AI. You're discussing it at Kinetic Live — but you haven't shipped it yet. We can help you get there before StarRez does."

Lead product: SectorPulse. Kinetic's strategic problem is not operational — it's intelligence. Barry Anns joined with a commercial mandate, but Kinetic's core product team needs to understand where the HE market is moving: PBSA demand shifts, university financial stress signals, regulatory changes in student welfare that affect accommodation requirements. SectorPulse (RAG over curated HE sector research — HESA data, OfS reports, HEPI surveys, UCAS trend data) gives the product and commercial team a competitive intelligence layer that no off-the-shelf tool provides with HE-specific framing. This is also the right conversation to open because it's low-friction: no internal data required, no IT integration, seats on a browser.

Key pain points: (1) Revenue & Funding Diversification (score 63) — the UK university sector is under enrollment and funding pressure; Kinetic's 85% penetration of a potentially shrinking customer base is a real strategic risk; Barry Anns needs sector intelligence to make the case for international expansion timing and private PBSA sector push. (2) Power Concentration & Governance — as a Constellation Software subsidiary, Kinetic's strategic moves are gated by Volaris capital allocation; internal reporting to justify acquisition targets (Bullet Solutions, HeyHub pattern) requires structured sector evidence. (3) Innovation & Product Development — the AI roadmap conversation is active at Kinetic Live level but no product has shipped; Staffroom-like workforce analytics integrated into KxStudent is a concrete AI feature that could differentiate against StarRez.

Buyer: Barry Anns (CEO, appointed mid-2024) is the right conversation — he came in with a commercial and growth mandate and is in listening mode for external partnerships. The Innovation Acceleration team is the internal champion for any AI features. Note that Volaris/Constellation's decentralised model means Barry has operational autonomy but capital decisions go up; keep the initial ask small enough that he can approve it without Volaris sign-off.

Watch out: Constellation Software subsidiaries are managed for ROIC, not growth at all costs. Any pitch must have a clear, near-term revenue or cost-reduction number attached. "AI strategy" conversations without a path to ARR impact will not get traction. Also: Kinetic's core market (UK universities) is under financial stress — position any investment as a response to customer cost pressure, not as a luxury capability add.

Catholic Schools Parramatta Diocese (CSPD)

Opening line: "You've just hired Patrick Ellis to lead Uplift across 80 schools — a man who ran the same job at CECG. He knows exactly what the data infrastructure problem is. The question is whether you build it or buy it. We've already built it."

Lead product: Staffroom. The December 2025 Patrick Ellis hire is the signal. He came from CECG's Catalyst evidence-based teaching framework — he has lived the exact problem Staffroom solves: managing teacher development, appraisal cycles, and professional learning hours across a large diocesan school network with fragmented HR data. CSPD's "Data Intelligence" EGM portfolio and the Uplift framework rollout mean there is now explicit leadership appetite for data-driven workforce management at the system level. Staffroom normalises TechOne HR exports against AITSL standards and surfaces a principal dashboard — a direct fit for an 80-school system mid-framework-rollout.

Key pain points: (1) Skills & Development (score 68) — 4,500 staff, 80 schools, NESA accreditation requirements, and an active Uplift framework rollout all require structured appraisal data that TechOne's HR module does not surface in a principal-usable format. (2) Quality & Consistency (score 72 for risk scoring) — Uplift requires consistent evidence-based teaching practice across 80 schools; without site-level data, central office cannot tell which schools are implementing Uplift well and which need support. (3) Financial Visibility & Governance — as a company limited by guarantee since 2023 with a new board governance structure, CSPD's BoardBrief needs are emerging; the compliance reporting to CSNSW and Catholic governance obligations are growing in complexity.

Buyer: Patrick Ellis (EGM Learning Outcomes) is the primary champion — he owns the Uplift rollout and has direct experience with the problem. Jack de Groot (CEO) is the strategic endorser. Jackson Chan (Head of Technology Services) is the integration gatekeeper — he needs to be in the room for any conversation about TechOne or FACES platform connectivity. Route in through Patrick Ellis directly; he will understand the product without a sales pitch.

Watch out: CSPD is a Catholic organisation with governance obligations to both the Diocese and government funders. Any product must have demonstrable alignment with faith-based educational values — not because it needs to be religious, but because the messaging must not feel secular-corporate or at odds with the "whole-person" development framing of Uplift. Also: procurement in a diocesan school system can be slow; the CSNSW collective agreements and the 2024–2029 funding cycle mean decisions often align with budget planning cycles. Aim to be in the 2026-27 budget window.

Acasus

Opening line: "Fenton Whelan has written publicly about AI as a governance and accountability problem — not a technology problem. We agree. The question isn't whether your Budapest data team can build the pipeline; it's whether your field consultants in Pakistan and Chad can get the insight fast enough to act on it. That's what we solve."

Lead product: SectorPulse. Acasus has a real Data and Engineering team (24 people in Budapest, confirmed Tableau and AWS S3 infrastructure) — they are not a no-tech consultancy. The gap is not infrastructure; it's synthesis speed. Consultants running education reform programs across 10+ countries need to brief government clients on cross-country evidence fast — what the OECD data shows about teacher deployment in comparable contexts, what World Bank evaluations say about the intervention they're recommending. SectorPulse (RAG over curated international education research) gives their field teams a cited multi-source briefing tool that the Budapest team doesn't have to build and maintain. This is a consultant productivity play, not a data infrastructure play.

Key pain points: (1) Power Concentration & Transparency (score 64) — government clients are increasingly demanding evidence-backed governance recommendations; Acasus consultants need fast access to cited sector evidence to support their advisory work. (2) Revenue & Funding Diversification (score 63) — with USAID funding at structural risk across the international development sector, Acasus is actively expanding its client base; intelligence on non-USAID funding flows (FCDO, World Bank, Gates Foundation priority shifts) is strategically valuable. (3) Financial Visibility (score 53) — grant compliance reporting across 10+ active country programs is a real administrative burden; GrantLens over donor agreements is a plausible second product once SectorPulse is established.

Buyer: Jonny Barty (CEO) is the current decision-maker — he has the mandate to "drive team growth" and is in active build mode. Ahmed Razzak (Head of Partnerships) is the commercial entry point for anything framed as a partnership. Joseph Marsico (Head of Swiss Office, technology specialist) is likely the internal advocate for any technology product. Fenton Whelan (Founder & Chair) is worth engaging if the conversation can be framed around AI governance and accountability — it's his intellectual territory and he will open doors that Jonny cannot.

Watch out: Acasus's funder mix is undisclosed but almost certainly overlaps with USAID priority geographies — the 2025 US foreign aid contraction is an existential risk for the sector. Any conversation that touches revenue or growth needs to acknowledge this reality; pretending it doesn't exist will kill credibility immediately. Also: Acasus is a consultancy with a strong internal delivery culture — any product must augment their consultants, not replace them. Frame as "your analysts get better evidence faster" not "AI does the research."

WISE — World Innovation Summit for Education

Opening line: "Dr. Kazmi came from the Gates Foundation with a mandate to move WISE from thought leadership to demonstrable programme impact. The WISE 12 theme was 'Humanity.io' — AI and human values. You can't credibly host a summit on AI in education without using AI in how you work. Let's talk about what that looks like."

Lead product: SectorPulse. WISE's core product is convening intelligence — bringing the right people together around the most important education ideas. Their research team (IIE-WISE AI consortium, 7 countries) produces substantial output, but synthesising it into actionable briefings for the summit programme, prize jury, and CEO's policy conversations is a manual research process. SectorPulse over WISE's own corpus (research publications, prize applications, IIE consortium reports) plus external feeds (UNESCO, OECD, World Bank) gives Dr. Kazmi and Dr. Ameena Hussain (Director of Programs) a briefing layer that makes the 35-person team punch at the weight of a much larger organisation.

Key pain points: (1) Power Concentration & Governance (implicit) — as a Qatar Foundation unit with sovereign funding but a new CEO under pressure to demonstrate impact, the governance reporting burden is growing; BoardBrief over QF reporting requirements is a second conversation. (2) Revenue & Diversification — WISE's new CEO is signalling Africa and girls' education as programme priorities; identifying grant and funding flows in these geographies requires fast sector intelligence. (3) Innovation & Research Synthesis — with 4,900+ prize applications since 2009, WISE sits on a substantial corpus of education innovation evidence that is effectively unsearchable; SectorPulse over that corpus is a product in itself.

Buyer: Dr. Asyia Kazmi (CEO) is the strategic decision-maker — she has the mandate and the Gates Foundation background to understand what good AI-assisted research tooling looks like. Dr. Ameena Abdul-Majeed Hussain (Director of Programs and Chief Curator) is the day-to-day champion who manages the research and prize programme. Rekha Zachariah (Administrative Coordinator) handles logistics. This is a small team (~36 FTE); the CEO will be involved in any meaningful procurement decision. Go directly to Kazmi; reference her Gates Foundation context and the IIE-WISE AI consortium as proof you understand the space.

Watch out: WISE has sovereign-level funding and no commercial revenue pressure — which means budget is not the obstacle, but strategic fit and brand alignment are. Dr. Kazmi will not adopt a tool that makes WISE look like it is outsourcing its intellectual work to a vendor. Any pitch must be framed as augmenting WISE's own research capabilities, not replacing them. The Qatar Foundation context also means procurement can involve multiple layers of institutional approval that bear no relationship to the actual urgency of the problem. Patience and relationship-building over several summit cycles is often how vendors land in this ecosystem.
